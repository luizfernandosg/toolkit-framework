# Problems, Theory of Change, and the Regeneration-Claim Boundary

The framework's purpose, stated as the problems it targets and the change it claims to support. (Source: master doc *Problems, Theory of Change, Outputs, and Outcomes*.)

## The problems

The core problem is **knowledge coordination friction**: useful knowledge already exists but is scattered across calls, chats, documents, wikis, repositories, podcasts, social media, local contexts, forums, governance archives, project directories, and people's memories. The framework targets ten facets of that friction:

1. **Shared knowledge is hard to find, understand, and use.** People repeatedly ask "where do I start, what do I read first, who is working on this, what already exists, what is mature?" — and the field is not yet organized well enough as a common resource.
2. **Coordination capacity is unevenly distributed.** Knowledge concentrates in a few high-context people who are already overloaded. The goal is not to replace human guidance but to make it more effective.
3. **The field is too complex for any one person or organization to hold alone.** It should develop shared orientation **without forcing uniformity** — communities keep their own language and priorities while becoming legible to one another.
4. **Aligned people, projects, and organizations are not discoverable enough to one another** — slowing team formation, peer learning, and collaboration (while preserving privacy, consent, and appropriate representation).
5. **Organizations need clearer documentation, theory of change, and memory** — without flattening local context into generic reporting.
6. **Knowledge is not consistently connected to action.** Many resources explain or list but do not help people decide what to do next.
7. **Complex systems thinking needs to become more accessible** — simplify where it helps, without pretending the work is simple.
8. **Trust, review, and maturity are unclear.** A raw note ≠ a reviewed guide; a polished AI summary ≠ human-reviewed understanding; a case ≠ a universal pattern; a public claim ≠ verified evidence.
9. **Implementation learning is being lost** in calls, chats, threads, grant reports, and scattered retrospectives.
10. **Structural integrity is named more easily than it is practiced.** A structure can look complete because its fields are filled in while hiding who holds power, what evidence supports a claim, or how failure would be detected.

## Theory of change

The change is **not**: *"we build a toolkit, therefore people benefit."*

The change **is**: *"we reduce knowledge coordination friction so people and organizations can move from confusion to clarity, from clarity to meaningful action, and from action to shared learning."*

If people are slowed by scattered knowledge, weak documentation, unclear pathways, unevenly distributed capacity, fragmented discovery, hard-to-navigate complexity, unclear review states, and lost implementation learning — and if the framework creates a **source-aware, review-aware, context-aware, implementation-aware knowledge commons** — then individuals orient faster and find where they fit; contributors and stewards guide more effectively from shared infrastructure rather than individual memory; organizations clarify their work and preserve memory; implementation learning becomes easier to preserve, compare, and return to the commons; and the broader field moves from scattered knowledge toward responsible action and shared learning.

## The regeneration-claim boundary

> **The Toolkit supports regenerative coordination. It does not guarantee regenerative outcomes.**

The framework does **not** directly regenerate ecosystems, communities, or institutions. It supports the **knowledge, documentation, coordination, review, and learning conditions** that help regenerative work become more legible, responsible, connected, adaptive, and field-informed. This boundary is load-bearing: it keeps the framework honest about what it can and cannot claim, and it routes regenerative ambition into evidence rather than assertion.

For regenerative claims to strengthen over time, the work must increasingly ask: *What is depleted? What is being restored? Who or what is affected? What mechanism connects the intervention to the intended restoration? What evidence would show improvement? What evidence would show harm, extraction, or failure? What learning should return to the commons?*

## Outputs and outcomes

**Outputs** (concrete things created): a public v1 site (front door — journeys, plain-language articles, knowledge map); a deeper master commons document; guided pathways/journeys; resource and source-system maps; concept explainers and systems-thinking resources; documentation templates for initiatives; review and maturity labels; feedback and learning channels; a resource appendix / source-system registry. *Inclusion in the registry does not imply endorsement or public-readiness.*

**Short-term outcomes:** people get clearer faster; human guidance becomes more effective; aligned people and projects find each other; organizations become easier to understand; missing knowledge becomes visible; review needs become easier to see.

**Medium-term outcomes:** reduced repeated-explanation labor; stronger contributor pathways; better organizational memory; improved cross-network coordination; more responsible use of tools and claims; higher-capacity contributors and stewards; stronger source-system care (living source systems distinguished from extractable link pools).

**Long-term outcomes:** a living regenerative knowledge commons; a stronger coordination field; more resilient organizations and networks; better alignment between knowledge and action; more structurally sound coordination systems (made *visible and reviewable* — not certified); more field-informed regenerative practice (where stronger regenerative claims require stronger evidence).

## The first practical test

The first test is **not** whether the whole architecture is complete. It is:

> Can real people bring their own context, interests, or questions and leave with more clarity, useful resources, relevant people, organizations, tools, and a meaningful next step?

A second test keeps the work grounded in learning rather than presentation:

> Can contributors, stewards, and reviewers identify what is missing, unclear, overclaimed, poorly sourced, or structurally weak?

## Lightweight learning signals

Evaluation stays lightweight — metrics support learning, they do not become the work. Useful signals: *Did this help someone get clearer? Find a relevant person, org, tool, or resource? Identify a meaningful next step? Help aligned people find each other? Help an organizer guide better? Help a high-context contributor see deeper system relationships? Help a group document itself? Reveal missing knowledge, unclear language, or a broken pathway? Surface something needing review or better source lineage? Distinguish raw material from reviewed knowledge? Prevent a claim from being presented with too much authority? Generate learning that improves the commons?*

The goal is not performance theater. The goal is to learn **whether the framework is reducing friction or creating more of it.**
